Resistance Training

Strength Work

3 sessions per week · 30–45 minutes · Protein smoothie immediately after

🕑 When: Training day mornings, 5:42am 💪 Phases: All phases, never interrupted
Why this matters neurologically: Resistance training builds the anterior midcingulate cortex (aMCC) — the brain region governing voluntary override of involuntary responses. The aMCC strengthens specifically through aversive voluntary effort: the last reps you want to skip, the set that is genuinely uncomfortable, the moment you choose to continue. This is the cortical region needed to override the misophonic threat response. The discomfort is not incidental — it is the mechanism.
Structure: 5 movement patterns, 2–3 exercises per session covering all patterns. 3 sets per exercise for beginners, building to 4. Rest 60–90 seconds between sets. The sequence matters less than covering all patterns across the week.
01 Squat Lower body ►

What it is

Bending at hips and knees to lower your body, then standing back up. Builds quads, glutes, hamstrings, and core. The single most important human movement pattern.

Beginner starting point

Box squatSquat to a chair or box, barely touch it, stand. 3 sets × 8–10 reps. Teaches depth without fear of falling.
Bodyweight squatOnce box squat is solid. 3 sets × 10–12. Arms forward for balance.
Goblet squatHold one weight at chest (dumbbell or water jug). 3 sets × 8–10. The weight helps you stay upright.

Form cues

  • Feet shoulder-width apart, toes pointed slightly out (15–30°)
  • Push knees outward — in the same direction as toes
  • Keep chest up, do not round the back
  • Lower until thighs are parallel to floor (or as low as comfortable)
  • Drive through the whole foot to stand — not just toes

Sets & reps

Beginner: 3 sets × 8–10. Build to 3 × 12, then add load. Intermediate: 4 sets × 6–8 with weight.

Progress when

When 3 sets × 12 feels easy with good form, add load or try a more challenging variation.

02 Hip Hinge Posterior chain ►

What it is

Pushing the hips back and hinging forward while keeping the back flat, then returning upright using the glutes and hamstrings. The foundation of all pulling from the floor.

Learn the pattern first

Stand one foot from a wall. Push your hips back until they touch the wall. That is the hinge. Feel how the hamstrings load — that is correct. Do not squat down. Do not round your back.

Romanian deadlift (RDL)Hold light weights at thighs, hinge back keeping weights close to legs. Hands reach mid-shin. 3×8–10.
Kettlebell swing (if available)Explosive hip hinge. Best learned from a video first. 3×10–12.
Deadlift (when form is solid)Start light. More demanding, more reward. 3×5–6 with load.

Form cues

  • Back stays flat — do not round at the lower back
  • Hinge at the hip, not the waist
  • Feel the hamstrings loading as you go down
  • Squeeze glutes to come back up, not lower back
  • Soft bend in the knees — this is not a squat

Sets & reps

3 sets × 8–10. The last 2–3 reps should require real effort. That effort is what builds the aMCC.

03 Push Upper body / chest / shoulders ►

What it is

Moving a weight (or your own body) away from you. Builds chest, shoulders, triceps. Divided into horizontal push (pressing forward) and vertical push (pressing overhead).

Exercises — Beginner to Intermediate

Wall push-upAbsolute beginner starting point. Hands on wall, lean and push. 3×10–15.
Incline push-upHands on a bench or step. Harder than wall, easier than floor. 3×8–12.
Push-upFull floor push-up. Hands slightly wider than shoulders. 3×6–10 building to 15+.
Dumbbell shoulder pressSeated or standing, press weights overhead. 3×8–10.

Form cues (push-up)

  • Body is a straight line from head to heels — do not let hips sag or pike up
  • Lower chest to 1–2 inches from floor before pressing up
  • Elbows track at 45° from body — not flared out to 90°
  • Squeeze glutes and abs throughout

Sets & reps

3 sets × 6–15 (depending on variation). Once 3×15 is achievable, progress to a harder variation.

04 Pull Upper body / back / biceps ►

What it is

Moving a weight toward you, or moving your body toward a fixed point. Builds the upper back, rear deltoids, and biceps. Often underdeveloped relative to pushing. Critical for posture.

Exercises — No gym required

Band pull-apartResistance band held at shoulder height, pull apart. 3×15. Accessible anywhere.
Inverted row (table or low bar)Lie under a sturdy table, grip edge, pull chest up. 3×8–10. Excellent pull alternative to pull-ups.
Dumbbell rowOne hand on bench, row weight to hip. 3×8–10 each side. Pull the elbow back, not the hand up.
Pull-up (if available)Full body weight. Start with negatives (jump up, lower slowly). 3×3–5.

Form cues (dumbbell row)

  • Back flat and parallel to floor — do not twist the torso
  • Drive the elbow toward the ceiling, not the hand toward the shoulder
  • Pause briefly at the top — feel the scapula squeeze
  • Lower slowly with control — do not drop it

Sets & reps

3 sets × 8–12. This pattern often needs more work than pushing — if in doubt, do an extra set of pulling.

05 Carry Full body / core / grip ►

What it is

Walking while holding a load. Trains the core and grip under real-world stress, builds total-body stability, and has disproportionate benefits relative to its apparent simplicity.

Exercises

Farmer carry (both hands)Pick up two heavy objects (dumbbells, bags of rice, water jugs), walk 20–30m. 3–4 sets. Moderate pace, tall posture.
Suitcase carry (one hand)One-sided. Harder on core — resist leaning toward the weight. 3 sets each side × 20m.
Overhead carryOne weight pressed overhead, walk. Advanced. Only when overhead pressing is solid.

Form cues

  • Stand tall — shoulders back, head neutral, ribs down
  • Do not lean to the side of the weight (suitcase carry)
  • Breathe normally throughout
  • Grip firmly but do not white-knuckle

Sets & reps

3–4 sets × 20–30 meters or 30–40 seconds. Load should be heavy enough that grip is challenged by the end.

Load suggestion

Beginners: whatever fills a shopping bag in each hand. Progress by increasing load or distance. The carry is done when form breaks — not when the timer ends.

Weekly template

Session ASquat + Pull + Carry
Session BHip Hinge + Push + Carry
Session CSquat + Push + Pull (lighter carry)
RestProtein smoothie immediately after every session. No berberine on training days.
Zone 2 Aerobic

Zone 2 & Waterfall

2 sessions per week · 45–60 minutes · BDNF peak ~2 hours post-exercise

🕑 When: Aerobic day, ~1:00pm 🏃 Venue: Waterfall preferred, any Zone 2 works
Why this matters: Zone 2 aerobic exercise is the primary non-pharmacological BDNF upregulator. BDNF peaks approximately 2 hours post-exercise — this is why CC sessions are scheduled ~2 hours after returning on aerobic days. The cold plunge adds a norepinephrine surge of 200–300% lasting hours, and activates the diving reflex for an immediate vagal tone boost. Schedule CC to land inside this combined window.

How to find Zone 2 intensity

The feelYou can hold a full conversation in sentences, but breathing is noticeably elevated. You would not want to sing. If you cannot speak in sentences: too hard. If you feel like you could do this all day with zero effort: too easy.
Heart rateRoughly 60–70% of your maximum. A rough estimate of max HR: 220 minus age. Zone 2 = that number × 0.6 to 0.7. For age 35: max ~185, Zone 2 = 111–130 bpm.
Duration45–60 minutes at this steady pace. The continuity matters — not intervals.
Feel testAt the end, you should feel pleasantly tired but not depleted. You should be able to carry a conversation within 5 minutes of stopping.

Waterfall Protocol

01
Outward climb: 45–60 min

Zone 2 climb to the waterfall

Maintain conversational pace throughout the climb. This is the aerobic work. Do not race. On the steeper sections where pace naturally forces you above Zone 2, slow down or pause briefly. Consistency of Zone 2 effort matters more than covering maximum distance.

02
At the waterfall: 3–5 min

Cold plunge

Enter slowly. Full immersion or as deep as conditions allow. Target 3–5 minutes. The first 30–60 seconds involve a cold shock response — shallow rapid breathing. Allow it to settle into slow deep breaths. That transition is the nervous system shift. The norepinephrine surge begins within the first 30 seconds and peaks around 3 minutes.

Do not hyperventilate before or during. If conditions are unsafe (high flow, unstable footing), skip the plunge and take the cold shower fallback.

03
Return walk: natural pace

Walk back

Natural pace. Allow body temperature to normalize. This is recovery time. Arrive home ready to eat, then rest before the CC session window opens.

Fallback when waterfall is unavailable: Any Zone 2 movement for 45–60 minutes (brisk walk, cycling, swimming, rowing) followed by a cold shower finish of 30–60 seconds. The session matters more than the venue. Do not skip the aerobic session because the waterfall is inaccessible.
CC scheduling on aerobic days: Return from waterfall, eat a small meal, rest for ~1 hour. The CC session begins ~2 hours after returning home. This places it at the BDNF peak. Do not rush the post-exercise rest — it is part of the protocol.
Morning Practice

Qi-Gong

15 minutes · Every morning · Begins Phase 1 Week 4

🕑 Training days: 7:15am (after somatic release) 🕑 Aerobic days: 6:00am (after somatic release)
Why this matters: Overnight somatic bracing (elevated shoulders, held jaw, shallow chest breathing) keeps the brainstem in low-level alert regardless of actual threat. The shaking practice physically breaks this overnight pattern in 3–5 minutes. The flowing practice trains the capacity to have strong somatic sensation without flooding — directly relevant to what CC requires from the nervous system.

Part 1: Shaking Practice

01
Duration: 5 minutes

Setup position

Stand with feet hip-width apart, soft bend in the knees (knees never locked). Spine relaxed, not rigid. Arms hanging loose at sides.

02
First 2 minutes: gentle

Begin bouncing from the knees

Small, gentle rhythmic bouncing — rising no more than 1–2 cm. Let the bounce travel upward through hips, belly, chest, shoulders, arms, jaw. You are not doing the shaking. You are letting the bouncing shake you. Keep everything soft — soft jaw, soft belly, soft hands.

03
Minutes 2–4: allow it to build

Let the shake amplify naturally

The shaking may intensify on its own — allow this. Arms may start swinging loosely, jaw may chatter slightly, hands may flap. This is the pattern being broken. Do not control or suppress it. If it feels odd, that is normal for the first week.

04
Final 1 minute: slow to stillness

Gradual deceleration

Slow the bouncing progressively over the final minute until standing completely still. Take three slow breaths. Notice the difference between how the body feels now vs one minute ago. That difference is the point.

Part 2: Flowing Practice

05
Duration: 10 minutes

Transition into flow

From the post-shaking stillness, begin slow intentional movement. There is no fixed routine. The principle: slow, fluid arm and torso movement that generates internal warmth and physical aliveness while the external appearance remains calm and controlled.

06
Movement options

Suggested movements to combine freely

  • Slow arm circles — one arm, then both, forward and backward
  • Torso rotation — arms trailing like wet cloth
  • Slow lateral side bend — arm overhead, arc to each side
  • Rising and sinking on the knees — like waves
  • Slow weight shift side to side, arms floating

Move at roughly half your normal pace. There is no wrong sequence. The quality is: internal sensation present, external appearance soft.

Minimum version (significant illness or fatigue): Shaking practice only, 3 minutes. Even on difficult mornings this 3-minute version breaks the overnight bracing pattern. Do not skip entirely.
Somatic Release

Somatic Release Protocol

Two versions — Phase 1 simplified · Phase 3 full sequence

🌱 Phase 1: 7–10 min · Morning 🌱 Phase 3+: 10–15 min morning · 20–25 min pre-CC
Why this matters: Chronic misophonic hypervigilance creates sustained defensive bracing: shoulders elevated, jaw clenched, psoas contracted, breath shallow. This muscular tension signals ongoing danger to the brainstem, keeping the nervous system in anticipatory alert regardless of whether any trigger is present. The somatic release protocol directly interrupts this feedback loop. Body tension at CC session start is one of the strongest predictors of session quality — the pre-CC sequence is not optional.
7–10 minutes · Every morning from Phase 1 Week 3. Run before the day's demands begin. Do not skip even when time is short — 7 minutes is sufficient.
01
5 minutes

Constructive Rest Position

Lie on your back on the floor (not a soft mattress). Knees bent, feet flat on the floor hip-width apart. Hands resting on your lower belly. Close your eyes. Allow the lower back to release toward the floor — do not force it, just stop holding it up. Breathe into your hands — feel the belly rise on the inhale. Jaw released, lips slightly parted. Eyes soft. Do not try to relax. Just stop efforting.

02
2–3 minutes

Diaphragmatic Reset

Keeping the constructive rest position, shift to counted breathing. Inhale slowly for 4 counts — belly rises, chest stays relatively still. Hold gently for 2 counts. Exhale slowly for 6 counts. On each exhale, allow the jaw to release slightly further. Allow the shoulders to drop. The exhale is longer than the inhale — this is intentional. The extended exhale activates the parasympathetic nervous system directly.

Morning (10–15 min): All steps at a calm pace. Pre-CC (20–25 min): Full sequence with more time in each step. Body tension at session start directly predicts session quality.
01
5 minutes

Constructive Rest

Lie on back, knees bent, feet flat, hands on lower belly. Lower back releases toward floor. Breathe into hands. Jaw released, eyes soft. Allow, do not force.

02
5 minutes

Psoas Release

From constructive rest, allow one knee to slowly drop outward to the side — do not push it down, let gravity take it. Hold for 2 full minutes. Then slowly bring it back to center. Repeat on the other side for 2 minutes. The remaining time: both knees together, hands on belly.

What it should feel like: A gradual releasing sensation deep in the hip and lower back, possibly spreading into the belly. Not a stretch. Not pain. Release.

If it produces anxiety or sharp pain: Return both knees to center and remain in constructive rest. Do not force.

03
3 minutes

Hip Circles

Stand up slowly. Feet hip-width, hands on hips. Begin slow circles with the hips — as large and slow as comfortable. 1 full minute in one direction. 1 full minute in the other direction. 1 minute of figure-eights. Move as slowly as possible. This is not exercise. It is the hip releasing accumulated compression from the psoas work.

04
3 minutes

Shoulder and Neck Release

Head semicircles (1 min): Slow head semicircles forward only — ear toward shoulder, chin toward chest, ear toward other shoulder. Never roll the head backward. Move at one-quarter normal speed.

Shoulder shrugs (1 min): Raise both shoulders toward ears, hold 3 seconds, then drop them completely. Repeat slowly. Each drop should be a release, not a controlled lowering.

Jaw release (1 min): Let the jaw drop open. Hold 5 seconds. Let it close. Repeat. Allow any tension held in the jaw to release. The jaw holds an enormous amount of nervous system tension.

05
3 minutes

Diaphragmatic Reset

Standing or sitting. Inhale 4 counts into the belly (belly expands, chest relatively still). Pause 2 counts. Exhale 6 counts with an audible, intentional release — a slow "haaah" is fine. On each exhale, allow the jaw to drop. Allow the shoulders to soften. Three rounds of this should be enough to feel a clear shift in baseline tension.

06
2 minutes

Integration

Stand normally. Notice the body without trying to change anything. Shake hands loosely at sides for 10 seconds. Take one slow breath. Scan from feet to crown: where is tension still held? You do not have to fix it. Just noticing it is enough to reduce it. The sequence is complete.

Important: Do not force sharp pain in any position. The psoas release should feel like release, not stretch. If any position produces significant anxiety, return to constructive rest and stay there for the remaining time. The simplified version is always available as a fallback.
